# Anti-AI Tone Checklist

Run every email through this list before scheduling. 10 rules. Takes 2 minutes.

## 1. Delete "I'm thrilled to..."
If the email opens with any variant of "I'm excited / thrilled / delighted to share", delete it. Your reader knows you're an autoresponder.

## 2. No em-dashes in body text
Em-dashes ( - ) are the single biggest AI tell in 2026. Use periods, commas, or restructure the sentence. One em-dash in a subject line is fine. More than one in body text is a flag.

## 3. No "It's not just X, it's Y" constructions
This pattern is AI catnip. Rewrite as two separate sentences or kill one of the halves.

## 4. No lists of three with parallel structure
"Fast, reliable, and beautiful." "Save time, save money, save your sanity." AI loves this. Break the pattern - use two items, four items, or vary the structure.

## 5. Specific beats general every time
"Weekly" beats "regularly". "12 minutes" beats "fast". "Q3 board meeting" beats "important meeting". If a number exists, use it.

## 6. One idea per paragraph
If a paragraph has two ideas, split it. AI writes dense paragraphs. Humans writing for inboxes write short ones.

## 7. The "read aloud" test
Read the email out loud. If you stumble, rewrite that sentence. Inbox readers skim, but the words still get parsed - awkward phrasing breaks trust faster than bad logic.

## 8. No "let's dive in" / "let's explore" / "let's unpack"
Three of the most common AI transitional phrases. Cut all of them. Replace with the actual next sentence.

## 9. The opener question test
If email 1 opens with "Welcome! Have you ever wondered how to...?" - rewrite. Newsletters earn attention by being direct, not by being friendly. First sentence should be a statement of value, not a softball question.

## 10. No emoji in subject lines (unless it's your brand)
Emoji in subject lines work for some consumer brands. For B2B newsletters they're an AI-tell and a professionalism tax. Skip them unless you have evidence your specific audience responds to them.

## Final check

Read the email on your phone. If it looks like a wall of text, add more line breaks. Inbox readers scan first - whitespace is structural, not decorative.
