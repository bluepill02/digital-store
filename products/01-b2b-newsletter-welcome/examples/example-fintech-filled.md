## Email 2 - Proof

**Subject:** From 14 days to 7.

A controller at a 240-person fintech told me last quarter: "We cut close from 14 days to 7, no new hires, no consultants. We just stopped re-doing the same work twice."

The thing they stopped re-doing: intercompany reconciliation. Every month they had two analysts spending 3 days each on a problem that came down to one bad mapping in the ERP.

Tally didn't fix the mapping. Tally just made the bad mapping visible, automatically, the moment the data flowed through. The two analysts now spend 3 hours a month on that reconciliation instead of 6 days.

If your team is spending 14+ days closing because of compounding reconciliation work, my guess is that 80% of the time is in 2-3 specific reconciliations. Most controllers know which ones. Few have measured them in the last year.

Which one is yours?

Reply and tell me. I read every response.

- Priya

## Email 3 - Quick Win

**Subject:** One measurement, this week.

You told me your close takes 12 days and nobody trusts the numbers until week 3. Before you evaluate any new tool, here's a 5-minute exercise.

Pick your single longest-running reconciliation. The one that always takes more time than you budget. Time it. Open a stopwatch, do the work, write down the actual hours.

Most controllers I ask haven't measured this in over a year. The number is almost always worse than expected, and "worse than expected" is the moment you get leverage to fix it.

Do this once before Friday and reply with the actual number. I'll feature the most interesting responses (with permission) in a future issue.

- Priya

## Email 4 - Origin Story

**Subject:** Why I left my controller job.

November 2022. I was a controller at a Series B fintech. I had just spent 9 days closing the books for October.

On day 9, my CFO asked me a question I should have been able to answer in 5 minutes: "What's our true revenue for last month, net of refunds and chargebacks, by product line?" It took me 4 hours to get a number I was willing to defend. By then the question didn't matter anymore - the board meeting was over.

I had been a controller for 7 years. The first 4 I loved. The last 3 I spent fighting spreadsheets. I'd become an Excel operator with a controller title, and the work I'd been hired to do - actually understand the business - had been pushed to the weekends.

I quit in March 2023. I started building Tally that week.

The thing I got wrong, for the first year: I thought the problem was bad software. The problem is that controllers are usually the only person in the company who understands how the money actually flows, and most tooling treats them as a back-office cost center instead of as the source of truth they are.

Tally is built around the assumption that the controller IS the source of truth, and the tooling should make their work visible to the rest of the company. We have a long way to go. But every other Thursday I write this newsletter because I think the work matters, and because the people reading it are the ones doing the work.

I write this on Tuesday nights. - Priya
