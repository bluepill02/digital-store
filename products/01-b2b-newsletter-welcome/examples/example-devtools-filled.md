## Email 2 - Proof

**Subject:** 90 minutes to 12.

Acme Corp runs 180 engineers across 14 timezones. Last quarter their VP Engineering told me they'd cut daily standup time from 90 minutes per day to 12. Same outcomes. No new hires. No process consultants. They just stopped having meetings about work and started having meetings about decisions.

The shift: every async update is a written Loomsy thread with one of three labels - "shipped", "blocked", or "decision needed". The "decision needed" ones become the only items discussed live. The other two don't make the meeting.

Most teams I've watched try this treat async as a writeup chore. The thing Acme got right is that they treat the written updates as the source of truth and the meeting as a followup. Inverting that one assumption saves 78 minutes a day at their scale.

If you're spending 90 minutes a day in standups and 80% of them produce no decision - what's the one part of your team's status update you could replace with a written thread this week?

Reply and tell me. I read every response.

- Sam

## Email 3 - Quick Win

**Subject:** One meeting, this week. 15 minutes. Reply with what happened.

You told me in the last email you spend 2 hours a day in standups that produce no decisions. Here's the one thing I'd try before spending a dollar on new tooling.

Run a 15-minute "decision-only" meeting with your team this week. Rules:

- No status updates. If someone starts one, redirect them to Loomsy (or Slack, or whatever).
- Only items that require a decision go on the agenda.
- The output of every item is either "decided", "deferred to <person> by <date>", or "needs more info from <person>".

The principle: meetings are for decisions. Status is free to be async. Most teams have it inverted.

Do this once before Friday and reply with what happened. Even if the meeting was a disaster - especially if it was - I want to hear it. I'll feature the best replies in a future issue (with permission).

- Sam

## Email 4 - Origin Story

**Subject:** Why I built this.

March 2024. A Slack message from an engineering manager I respect.

"Sam, I just spent 3 hours in a standup where 4 people gave status updates and we made 0 decisions. I can't keep doing this. I'm leaving."

I'd heard versions of this message 8 times that quarter. Three of those people left their jobs within 6 months. Not because of standups specifically, but because standups were the daily reminder that their job had become operations work instead of building work.

I had built two devtools before this, both acquired. I'd never built something because I was angry. Loomsy started angry.

The thing I got wrong, for the first year, was thinking the problem was the meeting. The problem isn't the meeting. The problem is that most teams use meetings as a substitute for thinking. Async standups don't fix that. Async standups just make the bad thinking visible, which makes it impossible to ignore.

I built Loomsy to make the bad thinking impossible to hide. The product is still rough around the edges. The team is small. We're not profitable yet. But every Tuesday I write this newsletter because I think the work matters, and because the people reading it are the ones doing the work.

I write this on Sundays. - Sam
